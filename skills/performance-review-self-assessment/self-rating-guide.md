# Self-Rating Guide

Reference for writing an honest, specific self-assessment. Use this when helping someone draft accomplishments, name challenges, and self-rate on the leadership principles.

## Core principle: Honest, not impressive

A self-assessment is not a sales pitch and not a confession. It's an accurate, evidence-based picture of your quarter that your manager reads alongside peer feedback before your review.

It fails in two directions, and both hurt you:

- **Inflation** protects your ego in the moment. It collides with peer feedback and manager observations, costs you credibility, and denies you the honest conversation you need to grow.
- **Underselling** feels humble but it's just as distorting. Wins you don't write down — with evidence — may not get credited. Modesty is not a gift to anyone.

The goal is **calibration**: claim what you earned, own what you're still building, and back both with specifics.

## Writing strong accomplishments (Section A)

Describe **outcomes**, not activity. Activity is what you did; outcome is what changed because you did it.

| Instead of (activity) | Try (outcome + evidence) |
|---|---|
| "Worked on the onboarding flow" | "Redesigned onboarding; activation rate rose from 48% → 61% over the quarter" |
| "Helped the team a lot" | "Unblocked 3 engineers on the auth migration, cutting an estimated week off the timeline" |
| "Improved performance" | "Cut p95 page load from 3.2s to 1.9s (–40%)" |
| "Did a lot of design reviews" | "Ran weekly design crits; shipped 4 features with zero post-launch design rework" |

For each accomplishment, be able to answer:
- **Impact** — who benefited and how (team, customer, business metric)?
- **Evidence** — what would your manager look at to verify it?

If a number genuinely doesn't exist, use a concrete observable instead ("zero rollbacks", "shipped on the committed date", "adopted by both pods").

## Naming challenges honestly (Section B)

A challenge is not an admission of failure — it's evidence of self-awareness and growth. The structure that works:

1. **What was genuinely hard** — a real obstacle, not a humblebrag.
2. **What you did** — your actual response, even if imperfect.
3. **What you learned** — the takeaway, or what you'd do differently.

Avoid the two traps:
- **Fake weakness** ("I care too much", "I work too hard"). These read as evasive. Name something real.
- **Blame** ("requirements kept changing", "QA was slow"). Even when true, the assessment is about *your* response. What did you do within the constraints, and what would you change next time?

## Self-rating the leadership principles (Section C)

Rate yourself on each of the six principles and give one specific example per rating.

### The scale

| Rating | What it means | Honest signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Role Model (RM)** | You exemplify this; others learn from your example | Rare. Reserve for the one or two principles where you're genuinely the example others follow. |
| **Demonstrates (D)** | You consistently show this in your work | The strong, normal rating. Most principles for most people land here. |
| **Developing (DV)** | You're growing in this area and working to improve | A sign of maturity, not a mark against you. Naming a real DV builds trust. |

### Calibration

- **Role Model is rare.** If you rate yourself RM on most or all six, the scale isn't doing its job — and the gap with peer/manager views will stand out. Ask yourself: "Would my teammates point to *me* as the example for this?"
- **A self-assessment with no DV** reads as low self-awareness. Almost everyone is genuinely developing somewhere. Finding it is a strength.
- **Every rating needs a story.** A rating with no example is just a vibe. Tie each to a specific moment this quarter.

### The six principles

1. **Ownership** — Takes responsibility for outcomes, leads up and down, proactively solves problems without being asked. Never says "not my job."
   - *RM:* others copy how you take end-to-end ownership. *D:* you reliably own your work and outcomes. *DV:* you sometimes wait to be told, or hand off problems you could carry.

2. **Transparency** — Communicates openly and honestly, shares context, surfaces problems early. Builds trust through candor.
   - *RM:* you set the standard for honest, early communication. *D:* you share context and flag issues. *DV:* you tend to sit on problems or under-communicate.

3. **Customer Obsession** — Starts with the customer and works backward. Deeply understands user needs and earns their trust.
   - *RM:* you pull the whole team back to the customer. *D:* you keep the user in mind in your work. *DV:* you default to internal or technical convenience over user need.

4. **Strive for Excellency** — Relentlessly high bar. Continuously raises standards for products, services, and processes.
   - *RM:* you raise the bar for everyone around you. *D:* you hold a high bar for your own work. *DV:* you're still calibrating what "great" looks like here.

5. **Be Curious and Open Minded** — Never done learning, seeks self-improvement. Explores new possibilities and acts on them.
   - *RM:* your learning visibly pulls the team forward. *D:* you seek out growth and new ideas. *DV:* you're building the habit of stepping outside what you know.

6. **Drive Results** — Focuses on key inputs, delivers with the right quality and timeliness. Rises to the occasion despite setbacks.
   - *RM:* you deliver under pressure in a way others learn from. *D:* you consistently land your commitments. *DV:* you're working on follow-through or prioritization.

## Common self-assessment mistakes

1. **Vagueness** — "Improved the product." Without specifics, it's invisible. Add the what, the impact, the evidence.
2. **Inflation** — Padding wins or rating yourself RM everywhere. It collides with peer feedback and costs you credibility.
3. **Underselling** — Leaving real wins off the page out of modesty. If you don't write it down, it may not count.
4. **Recency bias** — Only remembering the last few weeks. Review the whole quarter — calendar, shipped work, notes — before you write.
5. **Activity over impact** — Listing what you were busy with instead of what changed because of it.
6. **No growth areas** — Claiming you're a Role Model at everything. It reads as a lack of self-awareness, not strength.
